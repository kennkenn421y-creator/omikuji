About Omikuji

What is Omikuji?
Omikuji (御神籤) are random fortune-telling paper strips obtained at Shinto shrines and Buddhist temples throughout Japan. Visitors shake a wooden cylinder until a numbered stick falls out, then receive the correspondingly numbered slip. The tradition dates back over a thousand years.
What do I do if I get a bad fortune?
If your omikuji reveals a bad fortune (凶 or 末凶), the traditional practice is to fold the slip and tie it to a designated tree branch or wire at the shrine. This is said to "leave behind" the misfortune. You can then draw again. A bad fortune is not a curse — it is a gentle warning from the spirits to be mindful.
How likely is each fortune rank?
Probabilities vary by shrine, but a common distribution is: 大吉 (Daikichi) ~17%, 吉 (Kichi) ~35%, 中吉 (Chukichi) ~15%, 小吉 (Shokichi) ~10%, 末吉 (Suekichi) ~8%, 末大吉 (Suedaikichi) ~5%, 凶 (Kyo) ~10%. Our digital oracle uses a weighted random system that mirrors authentic shrine distributions.
Are the predictions on the slip accurate?
Traditional omikuji cover areas like love, work, health, travel, and finances — giving poetic, open-ended guidance rather than specific predictions. Our slips follow this same tradition: the messages are reflective and symbolic, inviting you to interpret them through your own circumstances.
Can I draw more than once?
At physical shrines, drawing multiple times in one visit is considered poor form — the spirits have already spoken. However, many modern Japanese people draw omikuji at New Year and at each major shrine visit throughout the year. For this online tool, draw as often as you wish for fun and reflection.
